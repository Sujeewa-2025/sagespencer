Over the past few weeks, I kept seeing the same thing pop up in my feed: people sharing pencil sketches of a face they'd never met, captioned with things like "this is apparently what my soulmate looks like." My first reaction was the same as yours probably is right now — a raised eyebrow. But the more I dug into it, the more interesting the whole thing became, so I figured it was worth writing up.

So what exactly is a "soulmate sketch"?

The idea is simple enough: an artist — in this case, a woman named Tina who's built a following around this — takes some basic information about you (your energy, your relationship history, what you're looking for) and produces a hand-drawn portrait of the person she believes you're meant to meet. No photo, no video call. Just a short questionnaire and, a little while later, a sketch lands in your inbox.

Whether you read that as "psychic ability" or "an artist with great pattern-recognition and intuition," the appeal is the same: it's a glimpse — real or imagined — at someone who might matter to you later.

Why it's spreading the way it is

I think it taps into something pretty universal. Most of us, at some point, wonder what's next — who's out there, what they look like, whether we'll recognize them when we see them. A sketch like this gives that question a face. Even people who go in feeling skeptical (myself included, at first) tend to come away saying the same thing: "okay, that was oddly specific."

"I wasn't expecting to feel anything about a drawing — but there was something about seeing a face looking back at me that made the whole thing feel a lot more real."
